A6 Architecture

Personalization engine — article ingestion and the shared KV contract

How articles and their classifications travel from the CMS into Sailthru and GAM today, why the current pipeline is a belt-and-suspenders artifact, and how a single page-KV contract replaces two parallel paths post-migration. Relevance signals on the reader side are covered separately in the Relevance model section.

Current ingestion mechanic

Today, article content reaches Sailthru by two parallel paths running simultaneously:

  • Drupal direct push. On every node save, Drupal calls Sailthru’s Content API (apiPost("content", item)) with the article payload and tag list. Push happens per-save, synchronous with publish.
  • Sailthru Content Library scraper. Sailthru independently crawls the rendered article pages and parses sailthru.* meta tags for title, tags, image, and description. Runs on its own cadence, asynchronous.

The scraper was the original ingestion path; the direct push was added as a reliability measure. Two paths in production today because neither was retired when the other was added — belt-and-suspenders. When they disagree (different tag sets, different timestamps, different image URLs), the last writer wins, which is usually the scraper.

Current GAM data path

GAM receives targeting data on a third path that doesn’t share format with either of the Sailthru paths.

  • Article page JavaScript pushes classification data into a dataLayer object on page load.
  • Google Tag Manager reads the dataLayer, applies any transformation rules, and emits key-value pairs into the GAM ad call via the GPT (googletag) API.
  • GAM matches the KVs against line-item targeting expressions and returns the winning creative.

Three paths, three formats. The same underlying classification (“this article is about liquid biopsy”) leaves the CMS three different ways — as a Drupal API payload, as a scraped meta tag, and as a dataLayer push — with no shared contract.

Post-migration mechanic

Post-migration collapses the three paths into one shared contract.

  • STAMP classifies the article against the governed vocabulary when Arc publishes (or on scheduled batch). STAMP writes classification records; Arc renders the page.
  • The rendered article page emits KV pairs as both meta tags (sailthru.*) for the Sailthru scraper and googletag.setTargeting calls for the GAM ad call. Same KV schema, two delivery mechanisms.
  • Sailthru Content Library reads the meta tags via the scraper. No more direct push; no more two-path reconciliation.
  • GAM reads the KVs directly via googletag.setTargeting. No dataLayer intermediate; no GTM transformation layer.
  • Junction handles user-profile writes (explicit preferences plus inferred signals) on a separate path. Content ingestion and user-profile updates are distinct workflows — Junction doesn’t sit in the content path.

One source of truth (page KV from STAMP), two consumers (GAM via ad call, Sailthru via scraper). The page becomes the contract.

The page KV schema

The schema is small, stable, and reusable. Every rendered article page emits:

Key Value shape Role
page_type article / category / job / frontpage Primary filter on every GAM line item. Drives which targeting branches apply.
tag Multi-valued; STAMP-produced slugs Per-article topic classification. Drives SOV sponsor matching and My Topics relevance alike.
subchannel Topical gating slug Coarser-than-tag grouping (editorial channel / vertical).
page_id Content identifier (single-valued) Article or category-hub identifier. Used for frequency capping and category-hub SOV.
topic STAMP-classified topic slug Reader-facing personalization match key; same vocabulary as tag.
sov-sponsor Active sponsor token; time-bounded Ephemeral; spins up and down with the campaign. Base vocabulary persists.

GAM’s Topic Takeover targeting lives on four to five of these keys plus the Sailthru-controlled newsletter_campaign key (set by email click-referrer URL param, not emitted by the article page). See the Competitor exclusivity section for the full TT targeting form.

BlueConic and other downstream consumers can read the same schema for other surfaces (paywall rules, on-site personalization beyond My Topics). The schema is specified once and reused.

Five wire-format divergences at cutover

The Drupal-to-Arc migration surfaces five concrete wire-format changes that must be reconciled at cutover. None of them is blocking; all of them require explicit engineering work.

Surface Drupal (today) Arc (post-migration)
sailthru.tags token format Display names (Next-generation sequencing) Slugs (next-generation-sequencing)
Tag cap on Sailthru output 15 terms max (hardcoded Drupal array_slice) Uncapped
Content Library push mechanism Direct API push per node save + scraper Scraper only
Per-tag user-interest writes vars.drupal_term_names_<site> arrays (Drupal-emitted) Junction customFields passthrough (no native Arc equivalent)
GAM emit path dataLayer → GTM → GAM Direct googletag.setTargeting

What each divergence means in practice:

  • Token format change. Sailthru segment rules that match on display names (Next-generation sequencing) stop matching when tags arrive as slugs. Every active segment rule referencing display-name values has to rebuild against slug values. 16 active Topic Takeover segment rules are in this bucket.
  • Tag cap lift. Drupal’s 15-term cap was an implementation quirk, not a product constraint. Lifting it means articles with fine-grained classifications get fuller coverage in Sailthru, and STAMP’s output isn’t silently truncated.
  • Ingestion path consolidation. Direct push retires. Content Library depends on scraper reliability alone. A scraper miss means an article doesn’t appear in the Content Library for that cycle — the direct push used to be the backstop.
  • User-profile writes. Drupal wrote follow-list updates straight to Sailthru vars.drupal_term_names_*. Arc has no native equivalent; Junction passthrough picks up this role. Junction’s customFields surface already accepts arbitrary vars writes, so no new gateway engineering.
  • GAM emit path simplification. Removing the dataLayer → GTM intermediate eliminates a transformation layer where drift has historically been hard to debug. Direct googletag.setTargeting is the GPT-native pattern and easier to observe.

What doesn’t change

  • Sailthru Content Library remains the content index that feeds the personalized brief. Schema on the Sailthru side is unchanged.
  • GAM targeting mechanics are unchanged. GAM reads KVs from the ad call and matches them against line-item predicates — no change in kind.
  • Zephyr template pattern on the Sailthru side continues as today. The daily brief still renders through a Sailthru-hosted data feed (feed ID 5d276693071eff43b11a8c47) at send time.
  • Segment-rule grammar on Sailthru is unchanged; specific rules rebuild with the slug vocabulary, but the rule structure (interest-threshold, ancestor list, suppression stack) carries over.
  • Ad-serving wire format to the client is unchanged — the browser still calls GAM the same way; GAM still returns creatives the same way.

Migration risks and considerations

  • Scraper reliability becomes load-bearing. With direct push retired, a scraper miss means an article isn’t in Content Library for that send cycle. Scraper latency also matters — a 4-hour scrape delay isn’t currently material (direct push covered same-day content), but becomes material post-cutover.
  • Segment-rule rebuild is load-bearing for revenue. 16 Topic Takeover segment rules drive per-send audience definitions. Any rule that references display-name values has to rebuild against slug values. Regression-test with paired pre/post rule outputs before cutover.
  • GAM line-item targeting audit. Line items targeting on tag values in display-name format (if any exist) need re-keying. The network-wide tag key has 663 values; 71 are live on GenomeWeb TT inventory. Any display-name stragglers need to be found and fixed before cutover.
  • Historical content backfill. Articles already in Content Library from the pre-cutover period have their old tag format. Decision point: re-scrape the backlog under the new schema, or accept that historical content carries legacy tags until it ages out of the send window.
  • GTM retirement scope. If GTM is used by other brands’ page implementations or for non-GAM purposes, retiring its GAM emit path doesn’t mean retiring GTM entirely. Scope to the GAM path only.

Open questions

  • Scraper SLA. What the acceptable scrape latency is for freshly-published content, and whether a monitoring path needs to alert on scraper misses. Engineering + marketing automation call.
  • KV schema governance. Who owns changes to the schema (adding a new key, renaming a value) once it’s in production. If BlueConic starts consuming the schema too, governance broadens.
  • Backfill decision. Re-scrape historical content or accept legacy-tag tail. Depends on how long the Sailthru send window looks back and how much historical inventory sponsor segment rules pull from.
  • Cross-brand schema alignment. If other Crain brands adopt the same page-KV contract, whether the schema is standardized cross-brand (cleaner, harder to onboard) or brand-specific (easier per-brand, fragments analytics).
  • Category-hub SOV mechanics. Today, page_id IS (taxonomy-NNNN) on the 100% SOV tier targets specific Drupal category hubs. Whether Arc preserves those taxonomy node IDs or provides a mapping table is an engineering scoping call — affects category-hub sponsorships specifically.
Net change: three parallel paths out of the CMS (direct push, scraper, dataLayer/GTM) collapse into one shared contract (page-emitted KVs). The “engine” stops being a hidden Zephyr template that only a couple of people can read and becomes a small, stable schema visible on every rendered article page, consumed by GAM and Sailthru alike.
A7 Architecture

Competitor exclusivity — today and post-migration

How sponsor competitor exclusion is enforced today, why the hand-maintenance model drifts, and how package ownership replaces it structurally.

Current mechanic

Competitor exclusion on a live Topic Takeover is enforced by a hand-maintained IS_NOT clause on each delivering line item’s targeting expression. The clause lists the sponsor tokens (<competitor>_topic_takeover) the current sponsor wants to be excluded from — one inline list per line item, mutated in place every time the competitive set changes.

The full targeting form on a 100% SOV line item has the shape (page_type IS article AND tag IS [brand-tag]) OR (page_type IS category AND page_id IS [taxonomy-NNNN]), with an optional AND newsletter_campaign IS_NOT (…) clause appended for competitor exclusion. There is no labels layer, no audience-segment layer, and no central exclusion registry — the exclusion lives inside each line item’s targeting predicate.

Drift visible in production

Hand-maintenance across ~10 concurrent campaigns produces visible inconsistency. A snapshot across live line items in the same month:

Sponsor (live TT) Competitors excluded today Signal
Agilent · Inherited Disease 6 Richest exclusion set in production
Takara Bio 1 Under-maintained vs peers in the same month — drift, not different sold exclusivity
Exact Sciences 0 No live competitor exclusions observed

Same product, same month, three different exclusion footprints. The footprints don’t reflect different commitments made to each sponsor; they reflect that the update-every-live-LI sweep isn’t always done.

Values currently in circulation

Sixteen *_topic_takeover tokens are referenced across live line items’ exclusion clauses today, each corresponding to an advertiser with a recent or active flight:

nateracancer_topic_takeover, illuminaseqapp_topic_takeover, hamilton_topic_takeover, bd_scmultiomics_cc_bulletin, agilent_inhdis_topic_takeover, jj_topic_takeover, rnaconnect_topic_takeover, hamamatsu_topic_takeover, qiagentarseq_topic_takeover, agilentgenetictesting_topic_takeover, agilentctdna_topic_takeover, exactsciences_topic_takeover, brukerspatial_topic_takeover, labcorp_cc, ccbiorad, fmilbchannel.

Adding a new sponsor means (a) minting a new newsletter_campaign token and (b) updating every other active Topic Takeover’s IS_NOT clause so the new sponsor appears in their exclusion set where appropriate.

Per-launch blast radius

Each live Topic Takeover is implemented as three tiers (TTBL billboard, TT standard, 100% SOV) in triplicate across three ad units (gnwo, gpo, gdxo) — nine delivering line items per campaign-month. With ~10 concurrent takeovers in flight, a new launch implies:

  • 3 tiers × 3 ad units = 9 line items per campaign-month
  • 9 line items × ~10 concurrent campaigns = up to ~90 targeting-clause mutations per new TT launch
  • No automation, no diff audit, no change log — every mutation is a manual edit to a targeting expression in the GAM console

The drift table above is the direct fingerprint of this workload — at ~90 mutations per launch across ~10 concurrent campaigns, some exclusion clauses don’t get the update, and nobody has a sweep job that would catch it.

Ad unit and tier mechanics (reference)

For the full targeting pattern, all three tiers share the same page_type / tag / page_id structure; they differ by the ad-position scope they claim:

  • TTBL (billboard) — page_type IS (article, job) AND newsletter_campaign IS (<advertiser>_topic_takeover). Matches only when the reader arrived via the sponsor’s newsletter click referrer.
  • TT (standard tier) — (page_type IS article AND subchannel IS (…)) OR (page_type IS article AND tag IS (…)), with the IS_NOT exclusion appended.
  • 100% SOV — (page_type IS article AND tag IS [brand-tag]) OR (page_type IS category AND page_id IS [taxonomy-NNNN]), with the IS_NOT exclusion appended.

The IS_NOT mutation load is carried by the TT and 100% SOV tiers. TTBL doesn’t carry an exclusion clause because it targets on the sponsor’s own click referrer.

Post-migration mechanic

Exclusivity moves from predicate-level to package-level. A sponsor buys a standard topic package for a defined run period; for that period, the sponsor owns all 5 newsletter positions and the full display takeover on the package’s matched article inventory. There is no “competitor” concept inside the line item — the package has one occupant at a time, by definition.

At package handoff, the next sponsor inherits the same position ownership. Nothing mutates across sibling line items because there are no sibling line items maintaining their own exclusion views. The delivering line items persist at the package level; only the creative and the newsletter_campaign binding for the package’s run period rotate.

Governance moves from reactive per-LI hand-maintenance to sponsor-roster and package-calendar management. Ad ops owns which sponsor owns which package in which month, not which IS_NOT clause belongs on which line item.

What ad ops stops doing

  • Maintaining inline IS_NOT clauses on every live line item
  • Running the up-to-~90-clause sweep across concurrent campaigns every time a new TT launches
  • Hand-auditing for drift between peers (the Agilent-6 / Takara-1 / Exact-Sciences-0 mismatch goes away)
  • Minting a new newsletter_campaign token per sponsor and propagating it across other active TTs’ exclusion sets
  • Reconciling which competitor tokens a given line item should be excluding right now

Open questions

  • Cross-package conflicts. If sponsor A occupies Package X while direct competitor B occupies Package Y during the same period, package occupancy describes within-package exclusivity cleanly. Whether a thin cross-package policy is needed on top (e.g., “do not co-schedule these two sponsors across packages in the same month”) is an ad-ops governance call for a later scoping pass.
  • In-flight transition. Existing signed flights run out under current mechanics. During transition, both models coexist — legacy TTs continue with their IS_NOT chain; new flights sell into packages. Ad ops needs a simple rule for which flights they’re maintaining the old way and for how long.
  • Reporting surface. Today, proof of exclusivity is indirect (read the targeting expression). Under package ownership, proof is structural (check who owns the package). Whether sponsors want a direct reporting view (“you’re the exclusive sponsor on Package X for the month of …”) is a sales-ops conversation, not an ad-ops one — but it affects whether ad ops builds a simple roster dashboard or not.
Net change: competitor exclusion stops being a condition written into each line item’s targeting expression and becomes a structural property of package occupancy. The up-to-~90-clause per-launch sweep goes away. Drift — the fingerprint of hand-maintenance — goes away with it.
A8 Architecture

Relevance model — explicit and inferred signals

How the personalized brief decides which articles each reader sees, and how layering inferred reading signals on top of explicit preferences changes the ceiling without changing the opt-in surface.

Current mechanic

Today, the personalized brief’s relevance is bounded by the reader’s explicit follow list. When a reader clicks “Add to My Topics,” the term is appended to a flat array on the Sailthru profile (vars.drupal_term_names_cgweb). At send time, the brief’s Zephyr template renders against a Sailthru-hosted data feed (feed ID 5d276693071eff43b11a8c47); the feed selects newly-published articles from the Content Library whose tags match the reader’s follow list.

Sailthru’s Content Library also maintains a per-user tag-interest vector from accumulated reading behavior, but that vector’s primary job today is sponsor Interest List segmentation — the 1.1×–2.0× interest thresholds that define who qualifies for a Topic Takeover send. The reader-facing personalized brief is anchored to what the reader explicitly asked for; behavioral signal exists under the hood but doesn’t drive the daily brief in a reader-facing way.

Where today’s explicit signal comes from

Three opt-in surfaces feed the same flat follow array. Distribution of ~16,300 follow events over the past 11 months:

Surface Share of follow events Notes
Preferences UI (/my-topics) 85% Search-and-pick from the curated topic list
Topic landing page (/topic/{slug}) 9% “Add to My Topics” button, follow-while-browsing
Article page 6% Inline follow buttons on individual articles

Cumulatively: 12,891 stated followers, 721,708 user×topic relationships (~56 follows per active user), accumulated over the product’s life. Every follow is a deliberate click — no auto-assignment, no onboarding batch.

Why explicit-only has a ceiling

The explicit model works for the readers who use it — 3× flagship engagement on the sends that go out. What it doesn’t do is evolve with the reader.

  • Configuration drift. 87.5% of /my-topics visitors are one-and-done. Readers configure once and don’t return. Follow lists freeze at the reader’s interests-as-of-signup.
  • Staleness. A follow from 2018 carries the same weight as a follow from last week. There’s no decay and no recency signal.
  • Discovery gap. Readers can’t earn relevance on a topic they haven’t yet discovered. Emerging topics don’t accrue ranking from behavior — they have to cross the discovery threshold into an explicit follow first.
  • Configuration burden. Keeping the list fresh requires the reader to maintain it. The one-and-done behavior and the 0–7 new My Topics signups per month in 2026 suggest readers mostly won’t.

Post-migration mechanic — explicit plus inferred

The opt-in surface doesn’t change. The explicit preferences UI remains the only opt-in path; the act of configuring a preference is the act of subscribing to the brief. What changes is that reading behavior begins writing back.

End to end:

  • Arc renders the article page with STAMP classifications emitted as page key-values.
  • Junction — Crain’s existing gateway between Arc and Sailthru — captures reading events and passes structured updates to the user profile. Junction’s customFields surface already accepts arbitrary vars writes unfiltered; no new gateway engineering.
  • Sailthru profile vars accumulate both explicit preferences (what the reader picked) and inferred signals (what the reader reads) as separate, structured layers on the profile.
  • The matching engine is unchanged. The daily brief’s Zephyr template still renders against a Sailthru data feed that filters the Content Library against the profile. The difference is what the profile contains — a richer signal goes in, a better-matched article set comes out.

How the two layers combine

  • Explicit preferences anchor the floor. If a reader has opted into a topic, that topic stays in scope regardless of behavior. The reader’s stated interests are never overridden by the inferred layer.
  • Inferred signals expand the ceiling. Topics the reader consistently engages with rise in ranking even without an explicit follow. Emerging topics can earn their way into the brief without requiring the reader to discover them first.
  • Recency and decay become available. Inferred signals carry timestamps; stale behavioral interest can decay in a way stale explicit follows cannot (explicit follows require affirmative removal).
  • The reader doesn’t maintain the list. Explicit layer stays stable at whatever the reader configured; inferred layer evolves on its own.

What doesn’t change

  • The send-time matching engine — Sailthru data feed → Zephyr template → Content Library filter. Same pattern, same feed architecture, same render path.
  • The opt-in surface. The preferences UI stays as the explicit contract between reader and product.
  • The delivery channel. Daily brief email remains the single consumption surface.
  • The relationship between opt-in and subscription — consolidated to one flow (the Section 03 change), not split across three surfaces with separate subscription plumbing.

Open questions

  • Weighting and decay. How explicit follows and inferred signals blend when they disagree; how quickly inferred interest decays without reinforcement; whether the explicit layer gets a recency model of its own. Product + engineering to scope during the preferences-UI workstream.
  • Signal surface area. Which reading events count — click-through, full open, scroll depth, repeat visits, on-site dwell — and what Junction captures and writes. A function of what Arc’s page emission exposes and what the reader-privacy posture allows.
  • Transparency. Whether the UI ever surfaces inferred interests back to the reader (“we noticed you’re reading a lot about X — follow?”). A UX decision that affects perceived trust, not a mechanical one.
  • Cold start. New subscribers have no inferred signal. Whether the brief leans harder on explicit preferences during onboarding, or falls back to flagship-style editorial picks until inferred signal accumulates, is a delivery-logic call.
  • Cross-device and cross-session. Inferred signals are only as good as identity resolution. Whether authenticated reading behavior gets more weight than anonymous behavior, and how the system bridges them, is an engineering scoping call.
Net change: the personalized brief stops being bounded by whatever the reader explicitly picked and starts evolving with how the reader actually reads. The opt-in surface doesn’t change; the send-time matching engine doesn’t change; what changes is the signal on the profile, via a passthrough path (Junction) that already exists.
A9 Architecture

STAMP classifier and editorial governance

How article tagging transitions from per-article editorial hand-assignment against a 3,000-term vocabulary to automated classification against a governed ~300-term vocabulary, and how editorial’s role shifts from primary tagger to vocabulary governor.

Current workflow

Every article gets tagged manually on publish. The Drupal editorial interface exposes two tagging fields: field_primary_topic (single-value) and field_topics (multi-value). Editors hand-pick terms from a single shared topics vocabulary that holds ~3,000 accumulated terms across three co-hosted sites (GenomeWeb, 360Dx, PMO).

The workflow has no approval step, no tag retirement queue, no alias or merge tool. Once a term is in the vocabulary it stays; once it’s applied to an article it stays. Drift accumulates by default.

Governance ownership splits three ways today:

  • Executive editor owns vocabulary strategy and maintains the curated 128-term My Topics list (the subset exposed to readers as followable).
  • Editorial tagging lead owns day-to-day term application at publish time.
  • Ad ops owns Topic Takeover term selection for ad-campaign targeting — essentially a parallel vocabulary curation for the sponsor side.

There is no single source of truth for “what terms does the product recognize.” Three curated lists overlap imperfectly.

What hand-tagging costs

The per-article time cost is modest (seconds to a minute per article). The compounding cost is governance drift — the vocabulary gets progressively noisier with no retirement pathway. Evidence lives in the vocabulary itself:

  • 468 of ~1,065 unique applied tags (44%) have been used exactly once. Either one-off coinage that should have been merged into a sibling term, or accidental duplicates from inconsistent editor entry.
  • 184 of ~505 terms across the three curated lists have zero load-bearing evidence — no engagement, no follow, no active sponsor segment rule. Retireable candidates by definition, retained because retirement isn’t a workflow step.
  • Inconsistency across editors is unmeasured but likely material. No approval layer means two editors covering similar content pick different terms, without reconciliation.

See Vocabulary sizing below for the full cross-reference of how the ~3,000-term inventory collapses to ~300 load-bearing terms.

Post-migration workflow

STAMP becomes the classification engine. On publish (and/or on scheduled batch), STAMP classifies each article against a governed ~300-term vocabulary and writes classification records per article. Arc holds section-level taxonomy only — no controlled tag vocabulary in Arc, no editor tag-picker in Composer.

Classifications emit as page metadata key-values when the article renders. GAM reads the KVs from the ad call; Sailthru reads the KVs via the Content Library scraper. Same source of truth, two consumers — the shape documented in the Personalization engine section.

Editor role — reviewer and governor

The editor’s relationship to tagging shifts from author to reviewer.

  • On publish. STAMP produces classification suggestions; the Composer sidebar surfaces them; the editor confirms high-confidence classifications or overrides edge cases. Low-confidence classifications get flagged for review; high-confidence ones pass through.
  • Ongoing. Executive editor owns vocabulary governance — adding new terms as the field evolves, retiring terms that lose load-bearing signal, reconciling alias and merge decisions. What used to be an implicit, per-article decision becomes an explicit, periodic governance workstream.
  • Classifier tuning. Editorial partners with engineering to adjust classifier weights and confidence thresholds as STAMP’s behavior gets observed against the corpus. This is new editorial territory that didn’t exist in the hand-tagging model.

Throughput economics

STAMP classification cost is not a budget conversation at single-brand scale. At 16-brand scale it’s material but not blocking.

Scale Volume Direct LLM classification cost
GenomeWeb alone ~20–30 articles/day Sub-$1/day — not budget-meaningful
16-brand Crain network ~300–500 articles/day (estimated) ~$100–180K/year — material, not blocking

Editorial labor recovered from hand-tagging offsets classification cost at both scales. The economics are clearly favorable; the real constraints are classifier quality and governance discipline, not cost.

What this enables

  • Consistency across editors. Two articles covering the same topic get classified the same way. Hand-drift between editors goes away.
  • Vocabulary retirement as a native concept. Terms that lose signal drop out of the governed vocabulary; they no longer clutter the editor’s picker (because there is no picker) and they don’t clutter reader-facing surfaces.
  • Sponsor-side vocabulary coherence. Ad ops’ parallel curation for Topic Takeover targeting collapses into the same governed vocabulary editorial maintains; sponsor targeting uses whatever terms the classifier produces.
  • Cross-brand portability. The same classifier architecture applies to any Crain brand. Per-brand work is the editorial golden-set and vocabulary shape, not a rebuild — detailed in the Cross-brand portability section.

Open questions

  • Confidence threshold for auto-pass vs editor review. Where the cutoff lives affects editorial workload and classification accuracy tradeoff. Needs an observation period post-cutover to tune.
  • Governance cadence. Whether vocabulary review is quarterly, ad hoc as emerging terms surface, or continuous. Executive-editor call; editorial operations question.
  • Entity tags vs topic tags. STAMP’s taxonomy covers topics and entities (companies, products, people). Whether entity tagging follows the same governance pattern or needs a separate lane is an open structural question.
  • Historical reclassification. Whether STAMP re-runs over the pre-cutover article backlog or only tags new articles forward from cutover. Affects sponsor SOV targeting on legacy content and Content Library quality for personalization.
  • STAMP ↔ Arc handoff. Whether STAMP writes classifications back into Arc for display purposes, or the page template queries STAMP at render time, is an engineering scoping call that affects page-load mechanics and caching.
Net change: editorial stops being the tagger and becomes the governor. Per-article tagging time collapses; vocabulary drift stops accumulating; classifier output replaces editor judgment on the per-article call while elevating editor judgment at the vocabulary level. The 3,000-term vocabulary of historical drift gets replaced by a ~300-term vocabulary of earned, load-bearing evidence.
A10 Operations

Marketing automation — today and post-migration

How per-send bespoke assembly collapses into package scheduling, and why single-operator risk dissolves as a structural consequence.

Current role shape

Marketing automation carries the load-bearing assembly work for every Topic Takeover. The function owns two distinct workloads: per-campaign build (run once, when a sponsor onboards) and per-send assembly (run every time a campaign mails).

Per-campaign build (~6 hours per new sponsor):

  • Configure the newsletter-ads-tool S3 app with the sponsor’s client code. The app returns 5 newsletter position names and an ads-proxy URL for the creative trafficking vendor.
  • Build a persistent Sailthru Interest List: smart list, ancestor-scoped to All Users Lists - Marketing, 10–21 tag interests at a hand-tuned threshold (1.1×–2.0×), 4 canonical global suppression lists.
  • Build the sponsor’s archetype Topic Takeover template: subject line, data-feed binding, brand header/footer. Fifteen archetype templates live in production today, one per active sponsor.

Per-send assembly (~1.5 hours per send · ~100–150 sends/year):

  • Clone the archetype template for the specific send (Topic Takeover - KS - <Advertiser> <Date> Email N).
  • Carve a ~5,050-member audience list from the parent Interest List (new normal-type list created per send).
  • Mutate the parent Interest List’s segment query to append the new carve to the exclusion clause, so the next send doesn’t re-mail these recipients.
  • Upload creative; coordinate with custom content on article picks; QA and schedule.

Across 15 campaigns and ~120 sends/year, marketing automation carries the largest single function’s share of the ~240 hours/year total backend labor estimate.

Single-operator risk — where it lives

  • One modify_user on every 2026 TT archetype template edit. API-verified across all 15 active templates. If the operator is out, archetype edits stop; per-send builds stop.
  • Institutional knowledge lives in one head. Which tags at which threshold for which sponsor; which segment carve-outs historically landed on which parent list; which archetype subjects have tested well. None of this is documented.
  • No failover. No second operator with modify access and the institutional knowledge to build a carve correctly. A vacation, a turnover, or an illness stops sends.

Supporting tooling that compounds the risk

  • newsletter-ads-tool S3 app. Static SPA at crain-platform-genomeweb-prod.s3.amazonaws.com/newsletter-ads-tool/. GenomeWeb-only — no other Crain brand wires through it. Undocumented. Nobody currently on staff can explain why it exists or who would rebuild it if it broke.
  • ads-proxy URL pattern. Lives at the Drupal layer, resolving to GAM creative positions. Not GAM-registered; not documented in a runbook.
  • Per-send carve list accumulation. The <Advertiser> <Date> Email N carves never get deleted. They persist in Sailthru’s list inventory indefinitely, compounding year over year. The accumulated clutter makes future list maintenance progressively harder.
  • Segment-query mutation as the stability boundary. Every send rewrites the parent Interest List’s segment query to add the latest carve-out. The “persistent” Interest List isn’t actually persistent — it’s re-written weekly. No stable version of the segment exists at any point in time.

Post-migration role — scheduler, not assembler

The product simplification — standard topic packages replacing bespoke per-sponsor assembly — is the structural unlock. Marketing automation’s workload collapses as a second-order consequence.

  • Standing package templates. One reusable template per package, not one per sponsor. Sponsor creative swaps in; template body doesn’t rebuild.
  • Standing package audiences. One pre-tuned audience per package, maintained once. No per-send carve generation, no per-send segment-query mutation.
  • Package-level competitor exclusivity. Handled structurally by package ownership (the Competitor exclusivity section). No inline IS_NOT maintenance on the marketing-automation side either.
  • Per-send artifact count drops to near zero. Schedule the send. Upload the creative. QA. Done.

Why single-operator risk dissolves structurally

  • No bespoke per-send assembly means no per-send institutional knowledge required. Any marketing-automation operator can run a package send.
  • Standing templates and audiences mean no modify_user bottleneck on edits tied to any single operator.
  • Package definitions document what used to be in an operator’s head. Tag thresholds, audience shape, template structure — externalized as package spec instead of tacit build knowledge.
  • No segment-query mutations means no week-over-week reconciliation, no drift between “what the segment was” and “what the segment is now.”

What the function still owns

  • Scheduling sends against the package calendar
  • Sponsor creative intake, versioning, and upload into the standing template
  • QA of scheduled sends before they fire
  • Performance monitoring and troubleshooting (coordination with ad ops and engineering when anomalies surface)
  • Package-level maintenance — tuning audience definitions, template structure, send cadence across the package shelf

What retires

  • newsletter-ads-tool S3 app — no longer needed; ad positions are package-standard
  • Per-send carve list creation
  • Per-send template cloning
  • Per-send segment-query mutation on parent Interest Lists
  • Per-advertiser archetype template maintenance (replaced by per-package templates)
  • Per-advertiser Interest List tag-threshold tuning (replaced by per-package audience definitions)
  • Accumulated per-send carve clutter (existing artifacts can be archived once legacy flights complete)

What this doesn’t automatically solve

  • The product simplification is the unlock. STAMP + Arc on their own don’t shrink Sailthru-side per-send labor; the standing-package product shape does. This is why the architectural work and the product work have to move together.
  • Creative approval and sponsor intake processes are separate workstreams. Package scheduling doesn’t automate sponsor creative QA or the legal / brand safety review layer — those continue as they do today.
  • Legacy flight maintenance. Signed flights running out under current mechanics continue to need per-send assembly through their end dates. Single-operator risk doesn’t fully dissolve until the last legacy flight completes.

Open questions

  • Access model for standing package templates. Who can modify a package template, and under what change-management process. A governance design for the new equilibrium.
  • SLA for creative upload to send go-live. Under bespoke assembly today, lead time is a function of whoever’s building. Under packages, a standard lead-time commitment becomes possible — sales will want to know what it is.
  • Staffing under the new model. Whether the function reshapes around package count (one operator across all packages) or by brand (one operator per brand at scale). A headcount and ops-design question.
  • Transition sequencing. Order in which legacy flights wind down and which sponsors migrate first to package-based flights. Revenue-ops call with marketing automation consult.
  • Cross-brand scheduling. As other brands onboard the same package pattern, whether the marketing-automation function centralizes scheduling across brands or stays per-brand.
Net change: marketing automation stops being the bespoke assembly line and becomes the scheduling and creative-upload layer. The single-operator risk dissolves not because we hired a backup, but because there is no longer a bespoke process that requires a specific person to run. The workload reshapes; the headcount question reopens; the institutional knowledge externalizes into package definitions.
A11 Strategic frame

Cross-brand portability — what “GNW is wave 1” actually means

The architecture is designed for portability from the outset. GenomeWeb is the first implementation, not a one-off. Subsequent brands inherit the pattern additively.

The framing

Every architectural decision in this work — STAMP as the classification engine, Arc holding sections only, page-emitted key-values as the shared contract, Junction passthrough for user-profile writes, standing topic packages as the sponsor product shape — has been made with 16 brands in mind, not just GenomeWeb. GenomeWeb is the first brand to implement it end-to-end. The validation work happens here; the pattern travels.

This isn’t aspirational. The product primitives that make the sponsor side work — Topical Bulletin Sponsorships and Core Channels — already exist at 360Dx and PMO in the 2026 Media Kit with published rates. STAMP already runs in production on three other Crain brands (Ad Age, Hart Energy, Automotive News), classifying content against a user-needs taxonomy. The architectural components are already at Crain; what’s new is the end-to-end composition.

What travels brand-to-brand

  • The architecture. STAMP as the classifier, Arc as the CMS with section-only taxonomy, page-emitted KVs as the shared contract, Junction as the passthrough gateway, Sailthru reading via Content Library, GAM reading via page KV. Same composition, no per-brand rebuild.
  • The sponsor product shape. Standing topic packages as a bundle of Topical Bulletin + matched display takeover. 360Dx already has 7 Topical Bulletins on the shelf; PMO has its own. Packaging those into standing sponsorships is the same motion we’re making at GenomeWeb.
  • The reader product shape. Explicit preferences + inferred signals + daily personalized brief. No surface is GenomeWeb-specific; the same opt-in surface and delivery channel apply to any brand with a sizable subscriber base.
  • The classifier approach. LLM-based classification against a governed vocabulary. Works at single-brand volumes; scales linearly with article count.
  • The operational relief. Single-operator risk, IS_NOT churn, per-send carve accumulation — every brand that runs Topic Takeovers today has some version of these, and the same structural relief applies.

What’s per-brand work

  • Editorial golden-set. Training and tuning data for the classifier on the brand’s corpus. This is editorial work, not engineering.
  • Vocabulary shape. Each brand has its own earned vocabulary — different term count, different term-to-article ratio, different governance shape. GenomeWeb’s ~300 is specific to GenomeWeb.
  • Section tree. Each brand’s Arc section hierarchy reflects its editorial shape.
  • Package definitions. Each brand maps its existing Topical Bulletin + Core Channel roster into the standing package shelf. Package count and structure vary by brand’s product mix.
  • Governance ownership. Who owns vocabulary review, classifier tuning, and package definitions on each brand’s side.

Cross-brand economics

Cost dimension Scaling behavior Implication
Engineering investment One-time STAMP extension, Arc emission contract, Junction passthrough wiring. Built once, amortized across every brand that adopts.
Per-brand onboarding Decreasing per brand First brand (GenomeWeb) carries full validation cost. Wave 2 inherits the pattern; wave 3 benefits from wave 2’s editorial learnings, and so on.
LLM classification cost Linear with volume Sub-$1/day at GenomeWeb volumes; ~$100–180K/year across all 16 brands. Material at network scale, offset by editorial labor recovery.
Operational relief Compounds per brand Each brand that onboards retires its own version of single-operator risk, IS_NOT churn, per-send carve assembly.

What varies across brands

The pattern is portable; the specifics aren’t identical. Notable variance points:

  • Sponsor product mix. Some brands run Topic Takeovers today; some don’t. Some have custom offerings that don’t fit the standing package shape cleanly. Per-brand product scoping is required to map what exists to packages.
  • Audience size and engagement. GenomeWeb’s numbers (611 personalized brief readers, 12,891 stated followers, 15 active Topic Takeovers) aren’t representative. Some brands have order-of-magnitude larger audiences; some have different engagement shapes.
  • Editorial workflow and tagging culture. Hand-tagging practices vary across brands. Some have tighter vocabularies already; some have looser drift than GenomeWeb.
  • STAMP maturity. Ad Age, Hart Energy, and Automotive News already run STAMP classification on user-needs today. Extending STAMP to topic + entity classification on those brands is an incremental step, not a cold start.
  • CMS state. Some brands are already on Arc; some are migrating; some are further out. Per-brand rollout timing aligns with each brand’s CMS state.

Implementation cadence

  • Wave 1 — GenomeWeb. Validates the pattern end-to-end: STAMP topic classification, Arc section-only taxonomy, page KV emission, Junction passthrough, standing package product, personalized brief with inferred signal layering.
  • Wave 2 onward. Brand-by-brand rollout aligned with each brand’s Arc migration timing and its product / editorial readiness. Each wave shorter than the last as the pattern gets internalized and tooling gets reused.
  • Pattern documentation. GenomeWeb’s implementation becomes the template — architectural, product, operational — that subsequent brands adopt rather than re-derive.

Strategic implication

Engineering investment is bounded and single; editorial / product investment recurs per brand but at decreasing cost; operational relief and revenue simplification compound per brand onboarded. The economics favor aggressive rollout once the pattern is validated, not a slow one-brand-at-a-time pace.

Open questions

  • Wave 2 selection. Which brand comes next — a function of CMS migration timing, editorial readiness, and revenue opportunity. CTO and brand-leadership call.
  • Package-shelf standardization. Whether packages standardize across brands (a shared shelf, cross-brand portability for sponsors) or vary per brand’s Topical Bulletin roster. Revenue-ops and editorial decision.
  • Classifier training topology. Whether STAMP trains one cross-brand model with brand-specific vocabularies, or per-brand models. Engineering scoping with the STAMP product and engineering teams.
  • Governance topology. Centralized cross-brand product team, or brand-specific ownership with a shared platform group. Org-design question.
  • Revenue accounting. Whether pricing and sponsor contracts stay per-brand, or a sponsor can buy a cross-brand package. Revenue-ops call.
  • Cross-brand audience overlap. Readers subscribed to multiple Crain brands — whether inferred signals travel across brands (stronger personalization) or stay brand-siloed (cleaner privacy posture). Product and legal scoping.
Net frame: GenomeWeb is not a bespoke build. Every architectural decision being validated here is a decision that 15 other Crain brands will inherit. The engineering spend is front-loaded and amortized; the editorial and product work recurs per brand but benefits from pattern reuse. The case for the investment is proven at GenomeWeb and then repeats.
