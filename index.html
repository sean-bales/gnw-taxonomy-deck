02Sponsor process

Sponsor delivery collapses from five systems and five-plus handoffs to three systems and two-to-three handoffs.

Bespoke per-campaign assembly retires. Standing topic packages take its place. Same deliverables for the sponsor.

Current
  • Manual content tagging. Editorial tags every article in the CMS by hand.
  • Bespoke campaign assembly. Five systems, five-plus handoffs, external vendor in the critical path.
  • Per-send hand-built newsletters. Marketing ops carves audiences and clones templates per send.
  • Hand-typed competitor exclusions. Up to ninety edits across the live book per new launch.
Future
  • Automatic content tagging. STAMP classifies every article on publish against the governed vocabulary.
  • Off-the-shelf package assembly. Three systems, two-to-three handoffs, no external vendor in the critical path.
  • Standing-template newsletters. No per-send carving, cloning, or template mutation.
  • Structural competitor exclusions. Zero exclusion-list edits per launch; the package owns all positions for the run period.
Critical-path systems 5 → 3. Per-campaign handoffs 5+ → 2–3. Per-send manual mutations → zero. Single-operator risk dissolves. See A2 and A3 for the system-by-system lifecycle.

Sources: ops audit of marketing automation, ad ops, and sales workflows; GAM line-item exclusion patterns; sponsor campaign register. Pulled 04/2026. Universe: 15 active GenomeWeb sponsor campaigns.

03Reader experience

Reader opt-in collapses from three surfaces to one, and relevance starts learning from reading behavior.

One opt-in surface. One channel. Automated relevance that improves with use. Same newsletter.

Current
  • Three opt-in surfaces. Preferences UI, topic-page button, article-page button.
  • Follow does not equal subscribe. Topic follow and newsletter subscription are separate plumbing.
  • Followable list mirrors the full Drupal taxonomy. Every term shows up in My Topics.
  • Personalization runs through an opaque Sailthru data feed. Tag-matching at render time inside an unauditable Zephyr template.
  • Relevance comes from explicit follows only. Reading behavior never feeds back.
Future
  • One opt-in surface. Preferences UI; topic-page and article-page buttons retire.
  • Follow equals subscribe. A single opt-in flow does both.
  • Followable list is generated automatically by STAMP. No editorial topic list to maintain.
  • Personalization runs through transparent page key-values. GAM and Sailthru read the same source.
  • Relevance comes from explicit follows plus inferred reading signals. The newsletter evolves with reading behavior.
Opt-in surfaces 3 → 1. Consumption surfaces 2 → 1. Editorial topic-list maintenance → zero. Pattern travels to 15+ brands. See A5 and A6 for the lifecycle in detail.

Sources: Sailthru user records and send logs; on-site preferences-UI analytics; Drupal taxonomy export. Pulled 04/2026. Universe: GenomeWeb stated followers (n = 12,891) and active-tier newsletter recipients.

04Architecture spine

Sponsor, editorial, and reader flows converge on one contract: page key-values emitted at article render.

The article page render is the only handshake. GAM, the Sailthru content scraper, and Junction all read from the same source.

  • One contract for content. Page key-values emit as Sailthru meta tags AND googletag.setTargeting calls.
  • One profile for the reader. Junction passthrough writes explicit preferences and inferred reading behavior to the same Sailthru profile.
  • One render pattern for the newsletter. Send-time match combines the reader profile against the indexed content library.
  • The same architecture stands up on every other Crain brand without rebuilding the spine.

Bucket 2 · Sponsor lifecycle

A2Sponsor lifecycle today

Today’s sponsor lifecycle runs through five systems, an external vendor, and ~90 hand-typed exclusion edits per launch.

Five systems. Five-plus handoffs. External vendor in the critical path. Single-operator risk on every send. The Sailthru-to-GAM bridge is a sponsor tag carried in the email URL.

Setup — what gets built once per sponsor
  • Sales hands the sponsor’s tag list and flight dates to marketing automation. The same sales record auto-generates draft GAM ad slots that someone has to finish setting up.
  • Marketing automation builds a custom audience by hand — smart segment over 10–21 content tags with a hand-tuned interest threshold — plus a sponsor-specific email template, plus a client code in a one-off in-house tool that exists only for GenomeWeb.
  • YourBow (external vendor) creates the actual delivering ad units in GAM. Ad ops then hand-types competitor exclusions onto each new ad unit — one inline list per unit, no central registry.
Per-send — what mutates every send
  • Marketing automation clones the template for each dated send and carves a ~5,050-member audience from the parent list.
  • The parent list’s segment then mutates in place so the carve isn’t re-mailed next cycle. The mutation persists; the parent isn’t a stable list at any point in time.
  • Carve lists are never deleted; per-send template clones accumulate cumulatively in Sailthru.
  • Newsletter curation contrast. Custom Content picks 3–5 articles per recommendation slot per Topic Takeover send, manual every send. The newsletter is 100% automated. Same content base, two operating models.
Send-fire — the URL handshake
  • No shared registry between Sailthru and GAM. The bridge is the email link.
  • Every link in the email carries a sponsor tag. The reader clicks; GAM reads the sponsor tag from the landing URL and takes over all 7 ad positions on the article page.
  • Competitor exclusion lives inside the GAM ad unit’s targeting expression, not as a Sailthru audience rule, because the bridge is the URL not a shared list.
  • If the URL parameter is wrong, mistyped, or stripped by an upstream link tracker, the takeover silently doesn’t fire and the sponsor sees house ads. Nobody monitors for that failure mode.
Maintenance and reporting — the ongoing tax
  • Steady-state cost: every new sponsor launch makes ad ops hand-type the new sponsor name into every other live sponsor’s exclusion list — up to ~90 edits per launch.
  • Visible drift between sponsor exclusion footprints (6 vs 1 in the same month) proves the sweep isn’t always done.
  • Revenue Ops pulls a monthly impression and click report and forwards to the sponsor.
  • Billing reconciles in the sales system against the original record, not GAM. Canonical revenue per campaign requires a cross-reference.
  • ~240 hours/year human labor on ~$1.1M declining revenue. The labor cost isn’t large in absolute terms; the fragility tax is.
Where the fragility lives. Single operator on every per-send carve and template edit. External vendor on every GAM ad unit with no internal backup. The in-house S3 ads tool is GenomeWeb-only and undocumented. Competitor exclusion lives inline in GAM targeting rather than as a shared list. The parent audience mutates every send, so no version of any sponsor’s audience is stable at any point in time.

Sources: marketing automation ops audit; GAM line-item targeting export; sales record cross-reference. Pulled 04/2026. Universe: 15 active GenomeWeb sponsor campaigns + ~10 historical Topic Takeover sends (six-send open/CTR mean).

A3Sponsor lifecycle with packages

Standing packages collapse the sponsor build to scheduling and creative upload, and competitor exclusivity becomes structural.

Three systems. Two-to-three handoffs per campaign. No external vendor in the critical path. No per-send manual mutations. Page key-values bridge Sailthru and GAM.

What changes structurally
  • Bespoke per-sponsor assembly retires. Standing packages mean one pre-tuned audience and one reusable template per package, not per sponsor.
  • External vendor exits the critical path. Pre-built packages don’t need bespoke per-campaign ad-unit creation in GAM.
  • Competitor exclusivity becomes structural. A package has one occupant at a time, by definition. The hand-typed exclusion list goes away with it.
  • The Sailthru-to-GAM bridge moves from URL handshake to shared page key-values. One source of truth for both consumers.
  • Per-send work for marketing automation collapses to scheduling and creative upload. Single-operator risk dissolves as a consequence.
Newsletter curation — explicit shift
  • Package-level editorial ownership replaces ad-hoc per-campaign curation. Editorial judgment lives where it matters: shaping what the package is and what it commits to.
  • No per-send Custom Content layer for sponsor sends. Article matching against the package’s vocabulary is automated, the same way the newsletter works today.
  • Editorial input is preserved at vocabulary governance and package definition. See A9 for the editorial-side picture.
What the sponsor still gets — unchanged
  • Same deliverables: monthly pageviews, e-blasts to readers interested in the topic, monthly report.
  • Same standing in the article inventory: full takeover on the package’s matched articles.
  • Same pricing structure (per the rate-card direction toward time-based packages, not pageview-based commitments).
  • Verification surface preserved — sponsors still see their topic landing in the wild.
  • Mid-flight transition: every signed flight completes under current mechanics. New sponsors sell into packages. No active campaign is disrupted.
Deliberately not committed in this slide. Exact package count, which current campaigns map to which packages, package pricing tiers. These get defined in a 2–3 week sprint with editorial and sales after architectural alignment. See A4 for the open questions.

A4Sponsor open questions

The sponsor workstream’s eight open questions are scoping conversations, not architecture blockers.

Each item is a question and, where applicable, a tentative direction. Most resolve in 2–3 week working sessions with editorial, sales, and ad ops.

  • Package count and structure. Tentative: start from today’s standing topical newsletters as the package shelf. 2–3 week working group with editorial and sales to define count, contents, and naming.
  • Mapping current campaigns to packages. Tentative: each current sponsor’s tag list maps to whichever package’s topic best fits. Sales and editorial co-own the mapping rules.
  • Package pricing tiers. Tentative: the internal rate card already directs the move from pageview-based to time-based pricing. Existing standard monthly unit holds through transition. Revenue Ops carries the detailed pricing pass.
  • Mid-flight grandfathering. Tentative: every signed flight completes under current mechanics. New flights sell into packages. No contract renegotiation. Needs a simple ops rule for ad ops on which flights they’re maintaining the old way and for how long.
  • Cross-package competitor co-scheduling. Within a package, exclusivity is automatic. Open: whether a thin policy is needed across packages. Ad-ops governance call for a later scoping pass.
  • Sponsor perception of the topic landing page. Sponsors today use the topic page as a verification surface. Open: whether the surface needs preservation, can simplify, or migrates to a sponsor-facing dashboard. Sales-led check.
  • SLA for creative upload to send go-live. Under bespoke assembly today, lead time is whatever the operator can manage. Under packages, a standard lead-time commitment becomes possible. Sales will want a number.
  • Headcount and ops design under packages. Open: whether the marketing automation function reshapes around package count, and what the right operator load looks like once bespoke per-sponsor assembly retires. Affects staffing plans.

Bucket 3 · Reader lifecycle

A5Reader lifecycle today

Three opt-in surfaces feed an unbounded follow array, but following a topic does not subscribe to the newsletter — which is why ~12,900 followers translate to ~625 daily sends.

Three opt-in surfaces. One unbounded follow array. A separate yes/no flag is the sole gate on the daily send. The send-time match is 100% automated and runs only inside Sailthru’s UI.

Three opt-in surfaces, one storage layer
  • All three follow surfaces (preferences UI 85%, topic landing page 9%, article page 6%) write to the same follow array on the Sailthru user record.
  • No auto-assignment. Every follow is a deliberate click.
  • Unbounded means any term in the ~3,000-term Drupal vocabulary can be followed — including long-tail terms that appear on a single article. The long tail is the point: niche topics are the product.
  • Average user follow count: 56. Unfollow-to-follow ratio: 1:21 — the array tends to grow.
The newsletter-subscription seam — the structural gap
  • Following a topic does not subscribe the reader to the daily newsletter. Newsletter subscription is a separate action that writes a single yes/no on the user profile.
  • The newsletter audience is gated entirely on that flag. No tag filtering inside the segment itself; matching happens at send time against the follow array.
  • Quantified gap: 12,891 stated followers → 2,181 emailable → 625 active-tier recipients per send.
  • This is the gap to close in the future state — one opt-in that subscribes the reader by definition.
Editorial → content index — the parallel pipeline
  • On every article publish, the editor hand-assigns a primary topic plus secondary topics from the shared 3,000-term Drupal vocabulary.
  • Drupal pushes the article and its tags into the Sailthru content index per save. Push is capped at 15 tags per article. The cap goes away in Arc.
  • The content index is the corpus every sponsor send and every newsletter filter against.
Send-time match — the 100% automated path
  • Each subscriber gets a uniquely assembled email. Sailthru matches content-index articles against the reader’s accumulated tag-interest signal (months of reading history).
  • Newsletter curation contrast. The newsletter is fully automated. Topic Takeover sponsor sends use the same automated tag-filtered feed but layer manual editorial picks (3–5 per slot) on top. Same pipeline, two operating models.
  • The match logic lives only in Sailthru’s UI — not introspectable from outside. Any upstream schema change that breaks the match silently degrades personalization without alerting anyone.
Why on-site doesn’t carry the value. 87.5% of preferences-UI visitors are one-and-done over 90d. Readers configure once and consume via email. The on-site feed is a setup tool, not a destination — which is why the future state retires the topic-page and article-page follow buttons but keeps the preferences UI as the single opt-in surface.

Acquisition is collapsing. New active signups Jan 2026: 11. Feb: 9. Mar: 8. Apr MTD: 2. The product is running on accumulated follow base, not fresh growth.

Sources: Sailthru user records and send logs; on-site preferences-UI analytics; Drupal taxonomy export. Pulled 04/2026. Universe: GenomeWeb stated followers (n = 12,891), emailable (n = 2,181), active-tier recipients/day (n = 625).

A6Reader lifecycle with inferred signal

One opt-in subscribes the reader; inferred signals layer onto explicit preferences without reader action.

One opt-in surface. One channel. Automated relevance that improves with use. Editorial role in the per-send picture: zero.

What changes for the reader
  • One opt-in surface. The preferences UI stays. Topic-page and article-page follow buttons retire; the on-site topic feed retires as a consumption surface.
  • Opt-in equals subscription. The seam between following a topic and subscribing to the newsletter closes. No second action required.
  • The reader doesn’t maintain a follow list. Explicit preferences anchor the floor; inferred signals from reading behavior expand the ceiling automatically.
  • Same delivery channel, same engagement profile. Newsletter remains the consumption surface; ~3× flagship engagement carries forward.
How the two signal layers combine
  • Explicit preferences anchor the floor. Opted-in topics stay in scope regardless of behavior.
  • Inferred signals expand the ceiling. Topics the reader consistently engages with rise in ranking even without an explicit follow. Emerging topics earn their way in without requiring discovery first.
  • Recency and decay become available. Inferred signals carry timestamps; stale behavioral interest can decay. Explicit follows still require affirmative removal.
  • Junction passthrough does the wiring. The gateway between Arc and Sailthru already accepts arbitrary profile writes. No new gateway engineering.
What stays the same on the Sailthru side
  • The send-time match pattern is unchanged: render against the reader profile, filter the content index, top-N digest per recipient.
  • The delivery channel is unchanged: newsletter email.
  • What changes is the signal on the profile (richer) and the source of classifications (STAMP via page key-values, not Drupal-pushed tags).
Cross-brand pattern. The same architecture — preferences UI + inferred signals + STAMP-classified content + newsletter delivery — supports a newsletter on any Crain brand. GenomeWeb is wave 1.

A7Reader open questions

The reader workstream’s six open questions are UX and signal-tuning calls, not architecture blockers.

Each item is a question and, where applicable, a tentative direction. Closing the follower-vs-subscriber seam is the only architectural outcome.

  • Weighting and decay between explicit and inferred signals. Tentative: explicit preferences anchor the floor (never overridden); inferred signals layer on top with recency-aware ranking. Tuning the blend lives in the preferences-UI workstream with engineering and product.
  • Which reading events count as inferred signal. Open: click-through, full open, scroll depth, repeat visits, on-site dwell. Function of what Arc’s page emission exposes and what reader-privacy posture allows. Engineering scoping with privacy review.
  • Transparency to the reader of inferred interests. Open: whether the UI surfaces inferred interests back ("we noticed you’re reading a lot about X — follow?"). UX decision; affects perceived trust, not the underlying mechanic.
  • Cold start for new subscribers. Open: whether the newsletter leans harder on explicit preferences during onboarding, falls back to flagship-style editorial picks until inferred signal accumulates, or both. Delivery-logic call.
  • Opt-in experience. End-to-end opt-in experience — surface, copy, sequencing, where the prompt lands — still needs work with marketing and product. Closing the follower-vs-subscriber seam is the outcome.
  • Topic landing page disposition. Open: whether the page persists for SEO and sponsor verification value, or simplifies into a sponsor-facing dashboard. Sales-led check with sponsors paired with editorial input on SEO impact.

Bucket 4 · Editorial & classification

A8Editorial today

Three parallel curation efforts feed one corpus, no shared source of truth, no retirement workflow — drift accumulates.

A 3,000-term shared vocabulary maintained by hand. Three parallel curated lists that overlap imperfectly. A per-send manual editorial layer on the sponsor side. No approval step, no retirement workflow.

Three governance lanes, no shared source of truth
  • Editorial leadership maintains a curated 128-term subset (the My Topics list, the followable surface readers see).
  • The editorial tagging lead applies tags per article from the broader 3,000-term shared vocabulary, every article, on publish.
  • Ad ops maintains a separate working list of which tags drive sponsor Topic Takeover targeting — a parallel curation effort.
  • The three lists overlap imperfectly. There is no single source of truth for what the product recognizes.
Newsletter curation — explicit contrast
  • Newsletter (My Topics Daily): 100% automated, no editorial curation per send. Sailthru matches content against each reader’s accumulated tag-interest signal.
  • Topic Takeover sponsor sends: automated tag-filtered feed PLUS a manual layer — the Custom Content team picks 3–5 articles per recommendation slot, every send.
  • Same content corpus, two operating models. Marketing automation has flagged that nobody can fully explain why the manual layer exists or why Custom Content rather than marketing automation owns it.
What hand-tagging costs — governance drift
  • The per-article time cost is modest (seconds to a minute). The compounding cost is governance drift.
  • 44% of unique applied tags have been used exactly once — one-off coinage that should have been merged, or accidental duplicates from inconsistent editor entry.
  • 184 of 505 terms across the three curated lists have zero load-bearing evidence — no engagement, no follows, no sponsor segment rules. Retireable by definition.
  • Inconsistency across editors is unmeasured but likely material — no approval step, no reconciliation between editors covering similar content.
The long tail is the point. Niche topics with small followings are exactly what the product is for; this isn’t an argument to shrink the vocabulary aggressively. It is an argument to govern it deliberately rather than letting it accumulate by default. See A9 for the future-state shape.

A9Editorial with STAMP

Editorial governs the vocabulary, STAMP classifies on publish, and one source of truth feeds reader personalization and sponsor matching.

STAMP classifies on publish. Editor confirms or overrides edge cases. Editorial leadership shifts from per-article tagger to vocabulary governor. One vocabulary serves both reader personalization and sponsor matching.

Editorial role shifts from tagger to governor
  • On publish. STAMP classifies; the editor confirms high-confidence classifications and overrides edge cases. Per-article time collapses.
  • Ongoing. Editorial leadership owns the governed vocabulary — adding new terms as the field evolves, retiring terms that lose load-bearing signal, reconciling alias and merge decisions.
  • Classifier tuning. Editorial partners with engineering to adjust classifier weights and confidence thresholds as STAMP’s behavior gets observed against the corpus. New territory.
  • Package-level editorial ownership replaces ad-hoc per-campaign curation. Where editorial judgment genuinely adds value — defining what a package is and what it commits to — editorial owns it. Where it doesn’t — per-send article picks — the manual layer retires.
One vocabulary, both jobs
  • The governed vocabulary serves reader personalization and sponsor matching from the same source.
  • Ad ops’ parallel curation for Topic Takeover targeting collapses into editorial’s governed vocabulary.
  • Sponsor packages match against the same classifications the newsletter reads from.
What this enables
  • Consistency across editors. Two articles covering the same topic get classified the same way.
  • Vocabulary retirement as a native concept. Terms that lose signal drop out of the governed vocabulary. The 3,000-term drift inventory stops accumulating.
  • The long tail is preserved deliberately. Niche topics aren’t dropped to chase a number; they’re retained because the product is for niche audiences. Governance is about earned signal, not size.
  • Cross-brand portability. Same classifier architecture applies to any Crain brand. Per-brand work is the editorial golden-set and vocabulary shape, not a rebuild.
Throughput economics
  • At GenomeWeb’s volume (~20–30 articles/day), direct LLM classification cost is sub-$1/day. Not budget-meaningful.
  • At 16-brand network scale (~300–500 articles/day), classification cost is ~$100–180K/year. Material but not blocking.
  • Editorial labor recovered from hand-tagging offsets classification cost at both scales. Real constraint is classifier quality and governance discipline, not cost.
Deliberately not numbered. The future-state vocabulary size is "governed," not "~300 terms" or any specific count. The shape gets defined by editorial governance once STAMP is observed against the corpus. See A10 for the open questions, including the company-name inclusion question that could move the working number.

A10Editorial open questions

The editorial workstream’s six open questions are governance cadence and STAMP-tuning calls; several need an observation period to settle.

Each item is a question and, where applicable, a tentative direction. Several depend on observation periods after STAMP runs against the corpus.

  • Confidence threshold for auto-pass vs editor review. Open: where the cutoff lives between high-confidence pass-through and low-confidence editor override. Affects editorial workload and classification accuracy tradeoff. Needs an observation period post-cutover to tune.
  • Vocabulary governance cadence. Open: whether vocabulary review is quarterly, ad hoc as emerging terms surface, or continuous. Editorial leadership’s call; an editorial-operations question.
  • Entity tags vs topic tags governance. STAMP’s taxonomy can cover topics and entities (companies, products, people). Open: whether entity tagging follows the same governance pattern or needs a separate lane.
  • Historical reclassification scope. Open: whether STAMP re-runs over the pre-cutover article backlog or only tags new articles forward. Affects sponsor matching on legacy content and newsletter recommendation quality during early days.
  • Company names in the controlled vocabulary. Editorial flagged that 50–100 company names sit on the boundary — they appear among the most-followed terms and the highest-engagement tags, and sponsor names are used as 100% share-of-voice terms today. Open: whether company names live in the governed vocabulary, in a separate entity lane, or both. Editorial leadership’s call.
  • Final vocabulary working number. Tentative: "governed," not committed to a count. The current working hard floor is 272 load-bearing terms; the company-name decision and the ad-ops audit on which tags are live in GAM targeting could move the number meaningfully. Final shape lands once STAMP is observed against the corpus.
