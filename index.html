A2.1 Sponsor lifecycle · current state

Sponsor lifecycle — end to end, today

Five systems, five-plus handoffs, an external vendor in the critical path, single-operator risk on every send. The bridge between Sailthru and GAM today is a sponsor tag carried in the email link.

Setup — what gets built once per sponsor
  • Sales hands the sponsor's tag list and flight dates to marketing automation. The same sales record has a parallel side-effect: it auto-generates draft ad slots in GAM that someone has to finish setting up.
  • Marketing automation builds a custom audience by hand — smart segment over 10–21 content tags with a hand-tuned interest threshold — plus a sponsor-specific email template, plus a client code in a one-off in-house tool that exists only for GenomeWeb.
  • YourBow (external vendor) creates the actual delivering ad units in GAM. Ad ops then hand-types competitor exclusions onto each new ad unit — one inline list per unit, no central registry.
Per-send — what mutates every send
  • Marketing automation clones the sponsor's template for each dated send and carves a ~5,050-member audience from the parent list.
  • The parent list's segment is then mutated in place so the carve isn't re-mailed next cycle. The mutation persists, so the parent isn't actually a stable list at any point in time.
  • Carve lists are never deleted; per-send template clones accumulate cumulatively in Sailthru.
  • Newsletter curation contrast. The Custom Content team independently picks 3–5 articles per recommendation slot per Topic Takeover send — manual, every send. The personalized daily brief (My Topics Daily) is 100% automated, with no editorial curation per send. Same content base, two very different operating models.
Send-fire — the URL handshake
  • No shared registry between Sailthru and GAM. The bridge is the email link.
  • Every link in the email carries a sponsor tag. The reader clicks; GAM reads the sponsor tag from the landing URL and takes over all 7 ad positions on the article page.
  • This is why competitor exclusion lives inside the GAM ad unit's targeting expression rather than as a Sailthru audience rule — the bridge is the URL, not a shared list.
  • If the URL parameter is wrong, mistyped, or stripped by an upstream link tracker, the takeover silently doesn't fire and the sponsor sees house ads instead. Nobody monitors for that failure mode.
Maintenance + reporting — the ongoing tax
  • Steady-state cost: every new sponsor launch makes ad ops hand-type the new sponsor name into every other live sponsor's exclusion list — up to ~90 edits per launch.
  • Visible drift between sponsor exclusion footprints (6 vs 1 in the same month) proves the sweep isn't always done.
  • Revenue Ops pulls a monthly impression and click report and forwards to the sponsor. Reporting is the cleanest part of the chain.
  • Billing reconciles in the sales system against the original record, not GAM. Canonical revenue per campaign requires a cross-reference.
  • ~240 hours/year human labor on ~$1.1M declining revenue. The labor cost isn't large in absolute terms; the fragility tax is.
Where the fragility lives: single operator on every per-send carve and template edit (sends stop if they're out) · external vendor on every GAM ad unit with no internal backup · the in-house S3 ads tool is GenomeWeb-only and undocumented · competitor exclusion lives inline in GAM targeting rather than as a shared list (no source of truth) · the parent audience mutates every send, so no version of any sponsor's audience is stable at any point in time.
A2.2 Sponsor lifecycle · future state future state

Sponsor lifecycle — with standing topic packages

Three systems, two-to-three handoffs per campaign, no external vendor in the critical path, no per-send manual mutations. Article page key-values are the shared bridge between Sailthru and GAM.

What changes structurally
  • Bespoke per-sponsor assembly retires. Standing packages mean one pre-tuned audience and one reusable template per package, not per sponsor.
  • External vendor exits the critical path. Pre-built packages don't need bespoke per-campaign ad-unit creation in GAM.
  • Competitor exclusivity becomes structural. A package has one occupant at a time, by definition. The hand-typed exclusion list goes away with it.
  • The Sailthru-to-GAM bridge moves from URL handshake to shared page key-values. One source of truth for both consumers.
  • Per-send work for marketing automation collapses to scheduling and creative upload. Single-operator risk dissolves as a consequence of removing the bespoke process.
Newsletter curation — explicit shift
  • Package-level editorial ownership replaces ad-hoc per-campaign curation. Editorial judgment lives where it matters — at the package level, in shaping what the package is and what it commits to — not in per-send manual article picks.
  • No per-send Custom Content layer for sponsor sends. Article matching against the package's vocabulary is automated, the same way the personalized brief works today.
  • Editorial input is preserved at the points where it adds genuine value: vocabulary governance and package definition. See A4.2 for the editorial-side picture.
What the sponsor still gets — unchanged
  • Same deliverables: monthly pageviews, e-blasts to readers interested in the topic, monthly report.
  • Same standing in the article inventory: full takeover on the package's matched articles.
  • Same pricing structure (per the rate-card direction toward time-based packages, not pageview-based commitments).
  • Verification surface preserved — sponsors can still see their topic landing in the wild.
  • Mid-flight transition: every signed flight completes under current mechanics. New sponsors sell into packages. No active campaign is disrupted.
What's deliberately not committed in this slide: exact package count, which current campaigns map to which packages, package pricing tiers. These get defined in a 2–3 week sprint with editorial and sales after architectural alignment. See A2.3 for the open questions.

A3.1 Reader lifecycle · current state

Reader lifecycle — opt-in, storage, delivery

Three opt-in surfaces feed an unbounded follow array, but following a topic does not subscribe to the newsletter. That seam is the structural reason ~12,900 followers translate to ~625 daily sends.

Three opt-in surfaces, one storage layer
  • All three follow surfaces (preferences UI 85%, topic landing page 9%, article page 6%) write to the same follow array on the Sailthru user record.
  • No auto-assignment. Every follow is a deliberate click.
  • Unbounded means any term in the ~3,000-term Drupal vocabulary can be followed — including long-tail terms that appear on a single article. The long tail is the point: niche topics are the product.
  • Average user follow count: 56. Unfollow-to-follow ratio: 1:21 — the array tends to grow.
The newsletter-subscription seam — the structural gap
  • Following a topic does not subscribe the reader to the daily newsletter. Newsletter subscription is a separate action that writes a single yes/no on the user profile.
  • The daily brief audience is gated entirely on that flag. No tag filtering inside the segment itself; the matching happens at send time against the follow array.
  • Quantified gap: 12,891 stated followers → 2,181 emailable → 625 active-tier recipients per send.
  • This is the gap to close in the future state — one opt-in that subscribes the reader by definition.
Editorial → content index — the parallel pipeline
  • On every article publish, the editor hand-assigns a primary topic plus secondary topics from the shared 3,000-term Drupal vocabulary.
  • Drupal pushes the article and its tags into the Sailthru content index per save. Push is capped at 15 tags per article. The cap goes away in Arc.
  • The content index is the corpus every sponsor sends and every personalized brief filter against.
Send-time match — the 100% automated path
  • Each subscriber gets a uniquely assembled email. Sailthru matches content-index articles against the reader's accumulated tag-interest signal (months of reading history).
  • Newsletter curation contrast. The daily brief is fully automated — no editorial picking per send. The Topic Takeover sponsor sends use the same automated tag-filtered feed but layer manual editorial picks (3–5 per slot) on top. Same pipeline, two operating models.
  • The match logic lives only in Sailthru's UI — not introspectable from outside. Any upstream schema change that breaks the match silently degrades personalization without alerting anyone.
Why on-site doesn't carry the value: 87.5% of preferences-UI visitors are one-and-done over 90d · readers configure once and consume via email · the on-site feed is a setup tool, not a destination — which is why the future state retires the topic-page and article-page follow buttons but keeps the preferences UI as the single opt-in surface.

Acquisition is collapsing: new active signups Jan 2026: 11 · Feb: 9 · Mar: 8 · Apr MTD: 2. The product is running on accumulated follow base, not fresh growth.
A3.2 Reader lifecycle · future state future state

Reader lifecycle — with inferred signal layering

One opt-in surface. One channel. Automated relevance that improves with use. Editorial role in the per-send picture: zero.

What changes for the reader
  • One opt-in surface. The preferences UI stays. Topic-page and article-page follow buttons retire; the on-site topic feed retires as a consumption surface.
  • Opt-in equals subscription. The seam between following a topic and subscribing to the brief closes. No second action required.
  • The reader doesn't maintain a follow list. Explicit preferences anchor the floor; inferred signals from reading behavior expand the ceiling automatically.
  • Same delivery channel, same engagement profile. Newsletter remains the consumption surface; ~3× flagship engagement carries forward.
How the two signal layers combine
  • Explicit preferences anchor the floor. If a reader has opted into a topic, that topic stays in scope regardless of behavior.
  • Inferred signals expand the ceiling. Topics the reader consistently engages with rise in ranking even without an explicit follow. Emerging topics can earn their way in without requiring discovery first.
  • Recency and decay become available. Inferred signals carry timestamps; stale behavioral interest can decay. Explicit follows still require affirmative removal.
  • Junction passthrough does the wiring. The gateway between Arc and Sailthru already accepts arbitrary profile writes. No new gateway engineering.
What stays the same on the Sailthru side
  • The send-time match pattern is unchanged: render against the reader profile, filter the content index, top-N digest per recipient.
  • The delivery channel is unchanged: daily brief email.
  • What changes is the signal on the profile (richer) and the source of classifications (STAMP via page key-values, not Drupal-pushed tags).
Cross-brand pattern: the same architecture — preferences UI + inferred signals + STAMP-classified content + newsletter delivery — supports a personalized brief on any Crain brand. GenomeWeb is wave 1.

A4.1 Editorial & classification · current state

Editorial & classification — tagging, curation, vocabulary today

A 3,000-term shared vocabulary maintained by hand, three parallel curated lists that overlap imperfectly, and a per-send manual editorial layer on the sponsor side. No approval step, no retirement workflow, no single source of truth for what the product recognizes.

Three governance lanes, no shared source of truth
  • Editorial leadership maintains a curated 128-term subset (the My Topics list, the followable surface readers see).
  • The editorial tagging lead applies tags per article from the broader 3,000-term shared vocabulary, every article, on publish.
  • Ad ops maintains a separate working list of which tags drive sponsor Topic Takeover targeting — a parallel curation effort.
  • The three lists overlap imperfectly. There is no single source of truth for what the product recognizes.
Newsletter curation — explicit contrast
  • Personalized daily brief (My Topics Daily): 100% automated, no editorial curation per send. Sailthru matches content against each reader's accumulated tag-interest signal.
  • Topic Takeover sponsor sends: automated tag-filtered feed PLUS a manual layer — the Custom Content team picks 3–5 articles per recommendation slot, every send.
  • Same content corpus, two operating models. The marketing automation lead has flagged that nobody can fully explain why the manual layer exists or why Custom Content rather than marketing automation owns it.
What hand-tagging costs — governance drift
  • The per-article time cost is modest (seconds to a minute). The compounding cost is governance drift.
  • 44% of unique applied tags have been used exactly once — either one-off coinage that should have been merged, or accidental duplicates from inconsistent editor entry.
  • 184 of 505 terms across the three curated lists have zero load-bearing evidence — no engagement, no follows, no sponsor segment rules. Retireable by definition.
  • Inconsistency across editors is unmeasured but likely material — no approval step, no reconciliation between editors covering similar content.
The long tail is the point. Niche topics with small followings are exactly what the product is for; this isn't an argument to shrink the vocabulary aggressively. It is an argument to govern it deliberately rather than letting it accumulate by default. See A4.2 for the future-state shape.
A4.2 Editorial & classification · future state future state

Editorial & classification — classifier + governed vocabulary

STAMP classifies on publish. Editor confirms or overrides edge cases. Editorial leadership shifts from per-article tagger to vocabulary governor. One vocabulary serves both reader personalization and sponsor matching.

Editorial role shifts from tagger to governor
  • On publish. STAMP classifies; the editor confirms high-confidence classifications and overrides edge cases. Per-article time collapses.
  • Ongoing. Editorial leadership owns the governed vocabulary — adding new terms as the field evolves, retiring terms that lose load-bearing signal, reconciling alias and merge decisions.
  • Classifier tuning. Editorial partners with engineering to adjust classifier weights and confidence thresholds as STAMP's behavior gets observed against the corpus. New territory.
  • Package-level editorial ownership replaces ad-hoc per-campaign curation. Where editorial judgment genuinely adds value — defining what a package is and what it commits to — editorial owns it. Where it doesn't — per-send article picks — the manual layer retires.
One vocabulary, both jobs
  • The governed vocabulary serves reader personalization and sponsor matching from the same source.
  • Ad ops' parallel curation for Topic Takeover targeting collapses into editorial's governed vocabulary.
  • Sponsor packages match against the same classifications the personalized brief reads from.
What this enables
  • Consistency across editors. Two articles covering the same topic get classified the same way.
  • Vocabulary retirement as a native concept. Terms that lose signal drop out of the governed vocabulary. The 3,000-term drift inventory stops accumulating.
  • The long tail is preserved deliberately. Niche topics aren't dropped to chase a number; they're retained because the product is for niche audiences. Governance is about earned signal, not size.
  • Cross-brand portability. Same classifier architecture applies to any Crain brand. Per-brand work is the editorial golden-set and vocabulary shape, not a rebuild.
Throughput economics
  • At GenomeWeb's volume (~20–30 articles/day), direct LLM classification cost is sub-$1/day. Not budget-meaningful.
  • At 16-brand network scale (~300–500 articles/day), classification cost is ~$100–180K/year. Material but not blocking.
  • Editorial labor recovered from hand-tagging offsets classification cost at both scales. Real constraint is classifier quality and governance discipline, not cost.
What's deliberately not numbered: the future-state vocabulary size is "governed," not "~300 terms" or any specific count. The shape gets defined by editorial governance once STAMP is observed against the corpus. See A4.3 for the open questions, including the company-name inclusion question that could move the working number.
A4.3 Editorial & classification · open questions

Open questions for the editorial workstream

Each item is a question and, where applicable, a tentative direction. Several depend on observation periods after STAMP runs against the corpus.

Confidence threshold for auto-pass vs editor review

Open: where the cutoff lives between high-confidence pass-through and low-confidence editor override. Affects editorial workload and classification accuracy tradeoff. Needs an observation period post-cutover to tune.

Vocabulary governance cadence

Open: whether vocabulary review is quarterly, ad hoc as emerging terms surface, or continuous. Editorial leadership's call; an editorial-operations question.

Entity tags vs topic tags governance

STAMP's taxonomy can cover topics and entities (companies, products, people). Open: whether entity tagging follows the same governance pattern or needs a separate lane.

Historical reclassification scope

Open: whether STAMP re-runs over the pre-cutover article backlog or only tags new articles forward. Affects sponsor matching on legacy content and the personalized brief's recommendation quality during early days.

Company names in the controlled vocabulary

Editorial flagged that 50–100 company names sit on the boundary — they appear among the most-followed terms and the highest-engagement tags, and sponsor names are used as 100% share-of-voice terms today. Open: whether company names live in the governed vocabulary, in a separate entity lane, or both. Editorial leadership's call.

Final vocabulary working number

Tentative direction: "governed," not committed to a count. The current working hard floor is 272 load-bearing terms; the company-name decision and the ad-ops audit on which tags are live in GAM targeting could move the number meaningfully. Final shape lands once STAMP is observed against the corpus.
